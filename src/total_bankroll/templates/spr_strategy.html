The Stack-to-Pot Ratio (SPR) is a crucial concept in Advanced Pot-Limit Omaha, particularly in understanding and applying the "Small Ball" and "3-betting preflop" strategies. It is explicitly introduced as a tool for thinking about Pot-Limit Omaha (PLO) and is considered even more useful in PLO than in No-Limit Hold'em (NLHE) due to PLO's bet-size restrictions and standard pot-sized betting.

Definition and Calculation

The Stack-to-Pot Ratio (SPR) is simply the ratio of the effective stacks to the current size of the pot.

  • For example, if you have a £1,000 remaining stack and there is £100 in the pot, your SPR is £1,000 / £100 = 10.
  • The effective SPR is always the SPR of the smaller stack in a heads-up confrontation.

What SPR Actually Means (in terms of bets)

SPR indicates how many pot-sized bets are left to play relative to the pot size.

  • An SPR of 1 means there is exactly one pot-sized bet left.
  • An SPR of 4 means there is enough money for two pot-sized bets heads-up, or a pot-sized bet and a pot-sized raise.
  • An SPR of 13 is the equivalent of three pot-sized bets heads-up.
  • If the effective SPR is over 13, and only two players contest the pot after the flop, the only way for all the money to go in is if someone puts in a raise at some point in the hand.

SPR Categories and Their Impact

The distinction between different SPR ranges is critical, as SPR has a dramatic effect on post-flop playing decisions.

  1. Ultralow-SPR Situations (SPR ≤ 1):
    • This is considered pot-odds territory.
    • You only need about 33.3% equity to justify an all-in confrontation when SPR is 1, and only 25% equity when SPR is 0.5.
    • You are virtually pot-committed if you catch any piece of the flop whatsoever.
    • You cannot fold hands like middle set, underfull, top two pair, bare nut straight with no redraw, or the second-nut flush, as you can beat many other hands for one bet.
    • In a low-SPR situation, you are essentially pot committed when holding the nut flush draw. Shove.
  2. Low-SPR Situations (SPR ≤ 4):
    • This is considered "shove-or-fold territory".
    • If you face a bet, you should generally shove all-in if you decide to contest the pot at all.
    • The range of hands you should be willing to commit with on the flop widens dramatically.
    • You should raise and commit your stack when facing a bet with "small-pot hands" (e.g., bare nut straight, middle or bottom set, top two pair, weaker wraps, undertrips, or top pair with redraws). These are hands you would rarely raise with when SPR is high.
    • In these situations, the penalty for being wrong (e.g., your opponent has a better hand) is not as steep.
  3. Mid-SPR Situations (4 < SPR ≤ 13):
    • This is considered the "Gray Area" or "Judgment Territory".
    • Play is more read-dependent.
    • If the SPR is in the low-mid range (closer to 4), you should lean towards treating the situation like a low-SPR situation (i.e., be more aggressive).
    • If the SPR is in the high-mid range (closer to 13), you should lean towards treating it like a high-SPR situation and proceed more cautiously (e.g., by smooth-calling rather than raising).
    • The updated SPR chart classifies SPR above 8 as Deep Stack Territory, and SPR between 8 and 13 as the mid-high-SPR range.
  4. High-SPR Situations (SPR > 13):
    • This is "Big Play (Implied Odds) Territory".
    • It is crucial to distinguish between "big-pot hands" and "small-pot hands".
    • Big-pot hands (e.g., nut straight with redraws, overfull, top set with redraws, nut flush, dominating draws) are universally strong, and you should "ram and jam" with them, aiming to get all the money in on the flop.
    • Small-pot hands (e.g., bare nut straight with no redraws, underfull, middle or bottom set, bare top two pair, undertrips, second-nut flush, sucker wraps/draws) should not commit their stack. It would be a "disaster" to commit your stack with these hands.
    • You should tend to refrain from raising on the flop with small-pot hands unless you have a hand strong enough to justify putting in a fourth bet; generally smooth-call when facing a bet.
    • The deeper the stacks and the higher the SPR, the bigger a mistake it is to commit your stack to small-pot hands.

Deep Stack Leverage and SPR

Deep stack leverage is the use of the threat of further bets to magnify the effective size of a bet, thereby creating fold equity.

  • It adds considerable value to big draws and dry-Ace bluffs.
  • It discourages raising and check-raising with marginal hands in deep stack situations.
  • A "deep stack" is defined as one that is deep enough to fire a legitimate third bet (i.e., a stack that allows for a pot/pot/half-pot sequence). This typically equates to an **SPR of about 8 or 9 or higher** (8.5 is enough for pot/pot/half-pot).
  • The actual "deepness" of a stack is determined by the SPR on the flop, not necessarily the starting big blind count. A 100BB stack might be deep in a passive game but not in an aggressive one.
  • The threat of the third bet is the source of leverage's power.

SPR in Strategic Plays

Small Ball

  • Small Ball is a **loose-passive preflop approach** that focuses on playing shorthanded pots with the positional advantage, but without the preflop initiative.
  • The goal is to utilise the positional advantage via the float to control pot size and opponents.
  • In deep stack scenarios (SPR > 8), if your opponent makes a continuation bet, you should **tend to call (float)** if you have a piece of the board or a nut draw.
  • You should **smooth-call with strong small-pot hands** (bare nut straight, middle set, top two pair, undertrips) to keep the pot size down and add weight to your floats.
  • The weaker your draw, the more dependent the value of your call is on the float.

3-Betting Before the Flop

There are two main strategies for 3-betting preflop, both heavily influenced by SPR:

  1. The Pump-and-Shove (Maniac/Low-SPR Play):
    • Involves a preflop reraise to create a **low-SPR situation (SPR ≤ 4)** after the flop, leaving two bets or less.
    • The idea is to build a big pot preflop. If the original raiser calls, you'll often be able to **shove all-in** on the flop if you catch any piece of the board.
    • This strategy is **volatile, high-volume, and has a relatively low edge**, requiring a larger bankroll than Small Ball. It also minimizes post-flop play, reducing the skill advantage.
    • For "trashy AA hands," the target post-flop SPR is under 1, meaning you aim to get at least one-third of your stack in preflop.
  2. Isolation Play (Tactical/Deep-Stack Play):
    • Involves a 3-bet to isolate the preflop raiser, aiming to play heads-up with the positional advantage in a **deep-stacked scenario (target SPR roughly 8 or higher)**. This SPR allows for three legitimate bets (pot/pot/half-pot) after the flop.
    • Advantages include:
      • **Leverage discourages opponents from check-raising** marginal hands, making them more predictable.
      • Enables the **float** as a viable weapon (SPR of around 8 is sufficient).
      • Gives you a **level of control over the hand**, allowing for smooth-calls, floats, and checking behind more often.
    • This strategy is also relatively volatile and difficult to play, making mistakes easy.

Key SPR Tips

  • The deeper the stacks and the higher the SPR, the bigger a mistake it is to commit your stack to small-pot hands such as a bare nut straight, middle or bottom set, bare two pair, or undertrips.
  • In a low-SPR situation (SPR ≤ 4), you should generally shove all-in if you contest the pot at all.
  • In ultralow-SPR situations (SPR ≤ 1), you are virtually pot-committed if you catch any piece of the flop.
  • The source of leverage tends to be the third bet.
  • A deep stack is one that is deep enough to fire a legitimate third bet, or about a half-pot sized bet on the river.
  • An SPR of 8.5 is enough to run the pot/pot/half-pot sequence.
  • A deep stack is not necessarily a 150BB or 200BB stack, but one that is deep enough to consistently see the flop with an SPR of about 8 or 9 or higher. In a passive game, a 100BB stack may be enough to constitute a deep stack.
